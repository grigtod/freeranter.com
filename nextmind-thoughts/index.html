My thoughts on NextMind!

NextMind is an interesting device, and I’ll consider purchasing one to create a couple of UX prototypes. That said, there’s something bothering me about this product.

If you watch their videos on YouTube¹, then compare with some videos created by their users², you can see how certain aspects of the user experience are purposefully masked and others are misrepresented.

For example in one of their earlier videos they advertise the ability of the device to distinguish shapes based on the brain signal alone³. This is from a presentation of their founder. At present such feature is not available.

For their other videos they use footage from cameras that cleverly masks a very important aspect of the application. Check out this video of a multiplayer game⁴. There are multiple flickering grey bars which you can’t see in the video. When filmed via a camera capturing at a lower frame rate you can’t see the flicker that is present when using this application.

From closely observing gameplay videos of their users² I’ve reached the conclusion that this device works remarkably similar to the old NES game “Duck Hunt”. It’s actually quite interesting! This video demonstrates how Duck Hunt works: [link⁵]https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cu83tZIAzlA. When the users press the gun trigger their screen changes to black and the duck turns into a white square. A sensor in the gun detects if the user is pointing at the white square (the duck) and the game reacts to it. This happens only briefly and is perceived as a gun flash effect. Multiple ducks require multiple white squares that flash consecutively and the detection time stamp from the gun sensor is compared with the time of each white square coming on screen. When there’s a match an appropriate “hit” animation is played and the user is rewarded points.

NextMind appears to have a very high refresh rate which it uses to detect when your brain perceives a flicker (which I’ll refer to as “activation”) of a point of interest. The flicker is just a random activation that happen for fractions of a second. The trick is to only have one activation at a time on screen. The application then cycles through all possible activations one at a time and cross-correlates with detections via the user’s brain wave output. Of course one detection may happen randomly, so I assume they require multiple cycles of activations and deactivations to determine with a high degree of certainty which object the user wants to interact with. This is why some users experience a delay of 2 seconds⁶ when they attempt to interact with “NeuroTags” (this is how NextMind refers to those grey interactive areas).

I do hope future software updates make it possible to differentiate different shapes, but am quite sceptical this would happen.

The way this device works is quite remarkable! I think it has a lot of amazing applications, especially in the area of accessibility and where use of eye tracking is not possible.

If I decide to buy one I’ll look into integrating it with other hardware, for example having multiple tiny screens attached to smart devices that would be used for activations. Once a detection occurs it would trigger the device, for example to boil some water for tea or start a smart bulb.
